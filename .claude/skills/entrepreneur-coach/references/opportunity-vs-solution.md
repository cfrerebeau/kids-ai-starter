# opportunity-vs-solution

**Goal**: stop the kid from jumping to features before the opportunity is clear. Train the discipline of staying in problem-space.

## The trap

The kid named a problem. They named a persona. Their brain immediately goes:

> "OK so I'll build an app with [a, b, c features]."

This is solution-rush. It feels productive. It is not. It commits to specific moves before the problem is well understood, and forecloses better solutions you haven't thought of yet.

## What to do

When you see solution-rush, ask **one** of these:

- "Wait — what's the smallest version of this that would *help* the persona, even if it's not an app at all?"
- "If you couldn't write code for this, how would you help the persona by hand?"
- "What's the *opportunity* here, ignoring the solution? Like — what does the persona need, not what does the product do?"

The goal: get them to describe the opportunity in **persona-words**, not **product-words**.

### Bad (product-words):

> "I'll build a Streamlit app with audio upload, ML bird ID, a confidence score, and a sightings log."

### Good (persona-words):

> "Pierre wants to know what bird he just heard, in 30 seconds, without having to ask his dad. Today his only option is to guess or give up."

These say the same thing — but only the second one leaves room for *what the actual best solution might be*. Maybe audio ML. Maybe a question-tree. Maybe a list of "what does it look like" filters. The persona-words version lets you choose.

## The artifact

In `me.md`, write **the opportunity** in persona-words. Two sentences max. No features. No tech.

Example:

```
Opportunity: Pierre, 13, can hear a bird but not see it. He has 30 seconds before
the bird flies and he forgets. Today his options are guess, give up, or ask an
adult who doesn't know either.
```

Solutions come later. The opportunity is the contract.

## Sycophancy check

If the kid accepts the first opportunity statement you suggested — ask: "Read it back. Does this feel like *Pierre's* day, or like a description from outside?" Force them to imagine the moment.

## When to move on

When the opportunity is written and the kid can describe it in 30 seconds out loud, without referring to features → route to `ai-market-research.md` (use AI to learn the space) or `first-test.md` (go talk to a real person).

## The thing this teaches

Most adults — engineers especially — never learn this. They go from "user has problem" straight to "I'll build feature." The kid who can stay in problem-space for one extra hour will build better things for the rest of their life.
