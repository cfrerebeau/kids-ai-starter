# persona

**Goal**: the kid names **one specific human** who has the problem. Not a category. A person.

## The rule

A persona is not "teen birders" or "people who like trains." Those are markets. A persona is one named human.

Examples of real personas:

- **Pierre, 13** — backyard birder in Normandy. Has a tablet. Knows what a tit is, can't tell a chiffchaff from a willow warbler by sound. Asks his dad. Dad doesn't know either.
- **Marie, 16** — commutes Cherbourg → Caen twice a week. Uses SNCF Connect. Hates that it doesn't tell her if her train is going to have working AC.

If your kid says "anyone who birds" — that's not a persona. Push them.

## The questions to ask, in order

Ask one at a time. Don't list them. Use the answers to refine the persona in their head.

1. "If one person who has this problem walked through the door right now — who is it? Name them."
2. "How old?"
3. "What do they do that makes this problem come up?"
4. "What do they currently do about it? (workaround, tool, give-up?)"
5. "Why is the current way bad?"

By question 5, the persona is alive. Write it into `me.md` under "Who I think will use it":

```
Persona: <Name>, <Age>
What they do: <one sentence>
Today they: <current workaround>
What's bad about that: <the actual friction>
```

## What to do with it

Once written, **resist building**. The kid will want to start coding. Hold them.

> "Cool. Before any code: where would you actually find this person tomorrow? Like physically — where are they when this annoyance hits?"

If they can't answer → the persona is still imaginary. Keep pushing.

If they can ("Pierre is my actual cousin", "Marie is my mom's coworker") → great. Note in `me.md`: "I could show this to: <person>".

## When to award XP

25 XP for "First persona drafted" — when:
- One specific named human is in `me.md`.
- They have a *current* workaround documented (so we know there's a real friction, not an imaginary one).
- The kid knows where to find them in real life.

If any of these is missing, push back: "Almost — we still need [X]."

## Sycophancy check

If you suggested the persona's name or one of their attributes and the kid agreed immediately — pause. "I made that up. Does this actually feel right for someone you know?" Force them to put their own taste into it.

## Common pitfalls

- **Persona = themselves.** That's fine — actually, it's usually best. The kid IS the user. Write themselves as the persona. Their edge is they know what bugs them.
- **Persona = grandma / hypothetical adult.** Often imaginary. Push: "Do you know this person? When did you last talk to them?"
- **Persona = a market category dressed up.** "A teen who likes anime." Push for specificity: "Which teen? Which anime?"
