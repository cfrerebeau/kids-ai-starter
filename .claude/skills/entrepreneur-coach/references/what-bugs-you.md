# what-bugs-you

**Goal**: extract a real problem from the kid's actual life. Not a "good idea" they had — a real annoyance, friction, or want they've personally experienced.

## When this fires

The kid has run a tinker example. They're warm. The session is going well. NOW you ask the question — not in onboarding, when they're cold.

## The question

> "OK, you've made the computer do something. What's something in your life lately that's annoying? Doesn't have to be deep. Could be tiny. School thing, friends thing, just life."

Give them a beat. Don't refine it. Wait.

## What good answers look like

- "I can never tell which bird is making a sound."
- "Train schedules suck on the app I use, especially for transfers."
- "I forget which homework is due when."
- "My friend group can't agree on a movie."

These are good because they have a specific situation behind them. The kid has personal experience of the friction.

## What bad answers look like

- "Climate change." → Too big. Ask: "what's one part of it that bugs YOU?"
- "Helping people." → That's a *want*, not a problem. Ask: "help with what specifically?"
- "I don't know." → That's fine. Offer alternatives: "What's the last time something pissed you off this week? Even small."
- "Make a million dollars." → That's an outcome. Ask: "by solving what problem?"

If they keep giving big abstract answers, give them an out: "It's OK to not have one today. Want to play with the examples more first and come back to this?" Don't force it.

## What to do with the answer

Once you've got one:

1. **Write it into `me.md`** under "My project" → "What I'm building" (as the *problem*, not the solution yet).

   Format: `Problem: <one sentence in the kid's own words>`

2. **Resist the urge to suggest a solution.** Don't say "you should build X." Say: "OK. So before we figure out what to build — let's get specific about who has this problem." Route to `persona.md`.

3. **Mark this as a quest milestone.** Update `quests/current.md` to the next step. Don't award XP yet — XP comes when the persona is drafted.

## Sycophancy check

If the kid's answer feels like a "cool idea" instead of a real annoyance ("an app that helps people learn languages with AI"), push back:

> "That sounds like an idea. What's actually annoying to you about learning a language? Like, what specifically did you try and fail at?"

The energy you want is grumpy and specific, not aspirational and broad.

## What this teaches

Two things, both core:

1. **Problems come before solutions.** This is the central entrepreneurship discipline.
2. **Personal experience is a competitive edge.** A kid building for a problem they *have* will outwork a kid building for a problem they *think other people have*.
