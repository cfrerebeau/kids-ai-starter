# first-test

**Goal**: the kid shows their thing to a real human and listens. This is the first non-imaginary feedback.

**Important**: the first human is NOT a stranger. It's someone the kid knows — a friend, a sibling, a parent. Strangers come later (`getting-users.md`), with coach approval.

## What "their thing" can be

Anything in this order of acceptability:

1. **A working prototype** — Streamlit app with the one feature.
2. **A clickable mockup** — slides or Figma showing screens.
3. **A drawing** — pen, paper, "here's how this would work."
4. **A description** — just the persona + opportunity + the one thing it does.

Lower is fine. Going from #4 to #1 doesn't get the kid better feedback — getting feedback at #4 saves them weeks of work.

## What to ask the kid before the test

1. "Who's the human?" → must be someone real, by name.
2. "Is this person close to the persona?" → ideally yes. A friend who shares the persona's situation.
3. "What's the one thing you most want to learn from this conversation?"

If the answer to #3 is "if they like it" → push back. You don't want to know if they like it. You want to know:
- What do they actually do today about [the problem]?
- Where does your thing fit (or not) in their day?
- What confused them?

## What the kid should NOT do

- **Don't pitch.** Pitching is selling. Selling biases the answer.
- **Don't explain how it works.** Show, ask, then shut up.
- **Don't argue.** If the person says "I wouldn't use this", *write it down*. Don't defend.

## The script for the conversation (kid's side)

Three sentences total:

1. "I'm working on a thing that helps [persona-style person] do [opportunity]. Can I show you?"
2. "Tell me what you'd try first."
3. After they try / look: "What confused you? What would make you not use this?"

Then the kid listens. Notes go on paper or in `me.md` under "Notes / journal."

## What "good" feedback looks like

- Specific: "I tried to click the button and it didn't do anything obvious."
- Behavioral: "Honestly I'd just google this. It's easier."
- Surprising: "I'd use this for [something the kid didn't think of]."

What's NOT useful:
- "It's nice." → ask: would you use it tomorrow? When?
- "Pretty cool." → push: what would have to be true for you to use it?
- "Yeah I'd use it." → suspicious. Ask: when's the last time you had this problem?

## After the conversation

The kid writes 5–10 lines in `me.md` under "Notes / journal":

```
First user test — <date>, <person's name>
What they did when shown: ...
What they said: ...
What surprised me: ...
What I'd change: ...
```

This is the artifact. Without it, the conversation didn't happen.

## When to award XP

50 XP for "First real conversation with a potential user" when:
- A specific named human had the conversation.
- Notes exist in `me.md`.
- The kid can name one surprise (something they didn't expect).

If the kid says "they liked it" with no specific quote or surprise → not yet. Ask for more.

## What this might trigger

- A persona change → route to `evolve-or-stick.md`.
- A scope cut → route back to `opportunity-vs-solution.md`.
- A confidence boost → great, route to `builder-coach` to actually build it.

## The *Receiver of No* badge

If the human said "I wouldn't use this" — celebrate. Award the *Receiver of No* badge. The kid just learned something invaluable: their idea, as currently designed, doesn't work for at least one real person. This is the moment most adults give up. The kid who continues becomes a builder.
